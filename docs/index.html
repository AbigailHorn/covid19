Predictive Epidemic Model for COVID-19 in Los Angeles County

University of Southern California, Department of Preventive Medicine

Updated with data available as of 2020-07-14


Aims

The USC Predict COVID project has developed the SEIR+Risk epidemic model to estimate and gain insight into the impact of COVID-19 in Los Angeles County and on its residents.

The primary questions we are addressing are:

  • When will the peak of the epidemic occur and how will it impact health care capacity?
  • What happens to the dynamics of the epidemic when social distancing is reduced or returns to pre-pandemic levels?
  • How will the epidemic affect different at-risk groups?
  • How will the epidemic affect different race / ethnicity groups?

Our epidemic modeling efforts provide:

  • Projections of illness severity trajectories in L.A. County as a whole and for race/ethnicity groups, based on prevalence of known COVID-19 risk factors.
  • Predictions for the impact on counts and corresponding time periods under various social distancing scenarios in which restrictions are eased.

The particular contribution of our model over existing models estimating hospital resource demand is that it accounts for combinations of risk factors (age, unhealthy behaviors, existing comorbidities, and combinations of comorbidities), including area-level differences in prevalence of these risk factors, in determining COVID intensity. Accounting for differential risk for specific populations is important both for making projections about the scale of the epidemic, and for prioritizing at-risk populations for protection. In the Risk Table page, we also provide the results of our estimates as a tool where individuals can look up their risk of severe illness based on their individual combinations of risk factors.

Probability of Severe Illness by Race/Ethnicity

This table shows the model estimated \(Pr(Hospital|Illness)\), \(Pr(ICU|Hospital)\), \(Pr(Death|ICU)\), and \(Pr(Death|Illness)\) by race/ethnicity group, as given by the prevalence of the risk factors in each race/ethnicity group in L.A. County. These probabilities are conditional upon an individual already having contracted COVID-19 illness, and so do not depend on the spatial distribution of exposure to the virus. These help to highlight whether a higher burden of mortality is expected for certain races for the same level of illness / hospitalization / ICU admission.

Although the nominal values of the probabilities do not differ significantly across races (for example, the probabilities for \(Pr(Death|Illness)\) vary from a minimum of 0.05 to a maximum of 0.14), the relative differences between the race/ethnicity groups may translate to meaningful differences at the level of the population. To visualize these relative differences, we have depicted these probabilities using color bars sized to a normalized scale for which the color bar for the largest probability value in a column spans the column width and the lowest probability for that column is shown with a very thin bar of color.

With the normalized scaling, this chart can be used to compare the probability of severe illness from COVID-19 across the race/ethnicity groups. Some insights this provides:

  • \(Pr(Death|Illness)\) is lowest for the Latinx population, largely attributable to the lower age distribution in this group.
  • \(Pr(Death|Illness)\) is highest for the Native Hawaiian popualtion, driven mainly by higher prevalence of smoking and obesity rates in this population.
  • \(Pr(Death|Illness)\) is almost equivalent for white and black populations.

Deaths by Race: Model Projections vs. Observed LA Data

This figure compares model projections of the relative share of deaths by race/ethnicity (right-most bar) with the observed relative share of deaths by race/ethnicity in L.A. County. The population prevalence of each race/ethnicity is also provided as a baseline (left-most bar).

A key insight from this figure is that as the epidemic evolves, the trend of the distribution of deaths by race/ethnicity appears to be converging to the expected distribution based on the risk factors for COVID-19, as calculated by our model.

However because we have not accounted for the many complex factors leading to differences in illness exposure by race, other beyond risk factors could be influencing the results we see here. An additional important note is that the observed distribution of deaths by race for L.A. County (columns 2 - 6 below) includes cases from nursing homes (SNFs), whereas the model projections are fit to data with these cases removed. Because SNF deaths represent a significant portion of overall deaths, removing SNF numbers from the observed distribution may lead to a different distribution of deaths by race for the general L.A. County population than that plotted below. This is demonstrated in the illustrative figures in the following section.

Death Rates by Race

Death rates by race: model vs. observed

Here we compare the relative risk (RR) of deaths per population for each race relative to deaths per population for whites, as given by our model based on risk factors for COVID-19 and the prevalence of these factors in L.A. County, with the observed COVID-19 illness and death rates in L.A. County.

Figure A (left) shows the observed rate of deaths per population by race relative to deaths per population for whites in L.A. County, i.e.

\[ \begin{equation*} RR_{White}(Death|Race_i)_{observed} = \frac{Deaths(Race_i)}{Deaths(White)} \ \end{equation*} \]

compared with the probability of death by race relative to the probability of death for \(race_i = White\), as given by our model based on risk factors for COVID-19 and the prevalence of these factors in L.A. County, i.e.

\[ \begin{equation*} RR_{White}(Death|Race_i)_{model} = \frac{Pr(Death|Race_i)}{Pr(Death|White)} \ \end{equation*} \]

Figure B (right) compares the observed rate of deaths given illness by race relative to deaths given illness for whites in L.A. County, i.e.

\[ \begin{equation*} RR_{White}(Death|Illness)_{observed} = \frac{\frac{Deaths(Race_i)}{Illnesses(Race_i)}}{\frac{Deaths(White)}{Illnesses(White)}} \ \end{equation*} \] with the probability of death given illness by race relative to the probability of death given illness for \(race_i = White\), as given by our model based on risk factors for COVID-19 and the prevalence of these factors in L.A. County, i.e.

\[ \begin{equation*} RR_{White}(Death|Illness,Race_i)_{model} = \frac{Pr(Death|Illness,Race_i)}{Pr(Death|Illness,White)} \ \end{equation*} \] Complex dynamics lead to exposure to COVID-19 and resulting illness. We have assumed a random-mixed model of transmission dynamics in L.A. and so do not account for these dynamics in our model. Because the probability \(Pr(Death|Illness,Race_i)\) is by definition conditional upon an individual already having contracted COVID-19 illness, it does not depend on these factors leading to exposure to the virus. We therefore compare this with the \(Pr(Death|Race_i)\), which will be determined by the nature of the distribution of exposure.

Interpretation

We see from this chart that the death rate relative to that for whites is predicted to be lower for Latinx population by our model (based on prevalence of risk factors for COVID-19); a decreased risk relative to whites is also observed in the data, both for deaths per population and deaths given illness. The death rate relative to whites for blacks is predicted to be almost identical to whites, and is observed to be slightly higher than whites both for deaths per population and deaths over illnesses.

Death rates by race: nursing home cases removed (illustrative)

Interpretation

A different story emerges when we remove from the observed numbers of illnesses and deaths the estimated numbers of counts originating from outbreaks occurring in nursing homes. Note that this serves as an illustration because exact numbers of illnesses and deaths by race are not available; for more details on how these cases are removed, see Data).

Because the majority of nursing home residents are white, once we remove the estimated counts from each race/ethnicity group from the overall numbers for deaths and illnesses, the relative share of deaths in the white population is decreased and all other shares are proportionally increased. The result is a picture of the risk of death relative to whites which is significantly higher for all other races, both for overall risk of death and the risk of death conditioned upon illness. These large differences in risk are apparent even after conditioning by illnesses, effectively conditioning out the component of the risk accounted for by differences in exposure and spread dynamics. In other words, after removing (an estimated number of) illnesses and deaths from nursing home residents from the data, all race/ethnicity groups are at significantly increased risk of death upon contracting the COVID-19 virus in comparison with whites. Most importantly, this significant increase in risk is not explainable by the risk factors for COVID-19 that are factored into our model: namely age, comorbidities, and health behaviors.
